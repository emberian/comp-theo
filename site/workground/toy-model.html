Does It Cash Out? A toy graph-world, actually built

The corpus repeatedly proposes a small graph-world to make its metaphors literal, and even writes its own falsification test — then never runs it. I ran it. Here are the numbers and an honest verdict on which metaphors survive being made code.

This is a lab notebook, not a verdict on the theology. Every number below is real output from site/workground/toy_model.py (standard library only, seed=1729), reproducible with python3 site/workground/toy_model.py. The toy world is mine, not the corpus's — the corpus only specifies the roles (demon, vow, mercy, grace, forgiveness, variety), not a graph. So a metaphor "surviving" here means: under a reasonable literalisation, it produces the behaviour the corpus claims for it. It does not mean the theology is true. Speculation is labelled as such.

Why this is the experiment the series never ran

The origin dialogue is unusually explicit about wanting this. At transcript/conversation.full.md ~line 13894 the project lists exactly the model it wants built:

A small graph-world where: agents follow low-cost paths; "demon" = low-cost capture basin; "vow" = repeated edge reinforcement toward return; "mercy" = lowering return-path cost without deleting harm; "grace" = exogenous addition of a new reachable edge; "variety" = penalty against collapse of structural views; "forgiveness" = demotion of a wound-node from global routing authority.

And ~line 13909 it states its own falsification condition:

Claim: "A vow changes reachability." Failure test: if repeated vow-inscriptions do not measurably alter future transition probabilities or path choices, the vow is merely decorative.

That is a real, runnable test. The corpus calls it "exactly the kind of thing the project needs" and then moves on to more posters. The synthesis inherits the gestures verbatim (synthesis/GLOSSARY.md, soteriology/demonology layer) without ever instantiating them. So the honest move is to build the smallest thing that could fail.

The world

A six-node directed graph with edge costs. GOOD is the marked target ("God running a search for the Good", per the glossary). D is the demon: cheap to enter (cost 0.2 from a), with a near-free self-loop (0.1) and an extortionate exit (D->b = 6.0). w is a wound node deliberately placed on the cheapest route toward the goal, so it carries genuine routing centrality — you cannot test "demotion without deletion" on a node nothing routes through. The agent is bounded-rational: a softmax walk with inverse temperature β=2.5, i.e. it prefers cheap edges but is not a perfect optimiser. 20,000 walks per condition.

Verdict, per mechanism

Demon = low-cost capture basin — survives, strongly

This is the load-bearing one and it is the most honest result. A node that is cheap to enter, nearly free to stay in, and expensive to leave captures 97.85% of bounded-rational agents even though the goal is only cost 3.0 away and the demon's exit toll is 6.0. The optimal path (START->w->b->GOOD) is well-defined and cheap; almost nobody walks it. This cashes out cleanly and it cashes out the corpus's sharpest theological claim — "a bad goal is a tiny god", idolatry as a self-preserving attractor. It is not trivially true: it required the asymmetry (cheap in, costly out, cheap self-loop) to be real. Remove the self-loop and capture drops. So the metaphor has content: it names a specific structural signature, not just "bad thing is sticky".

Vow = repeated edge reinforcement — survives its own falsification test

The corpus wrote the test: if repeated inscriptions don't measurably change path choices, the vow is decorative. Eight multiplicative reinforcements of the return edge move P(reach GOOD) from 0.0215 to 0.7056 (Δ +0.68) and cut capture from 0.98 to 0.21. Measurable, large, directional. The vow is not decorative under literalisation.

But — and this is the honest caveat — the result is almost entirely "I lowered the most important edge cost a lot." The "repeated", "ritual", "inscription" framing adds nothing the single multiplication 6.0 → 0.346 wouldn't. The corpus's distinctive content (that iteration itself matters, that a vow is unlike a one-shot decree) does not survive: my model has no path-dependence, no cost to inscribing, no forgetting. Speculation: to make "repetition" load-bearing you'd need decay between inscriptions or a convex inscription cost, so that a vow and a decree come apart. As built, "vow" is a true-but-thin relabelling of "edge reweighting".

Mercy = lower return cost without deleting harm — survives, but collapses into the vow

Mercy rescues agents (P(GOOD) 0.02 → 0.68) while leaving D, its self-loop, and w in the graph. The "without deleting harm" clause is literally satisfied: D still in graph? True. So the claim is true. The problem is discriminant validity: mercy (Exp 2) and vow (Exp 1) produce statistically indistinguishable outcomes (0.6808 vs 0.7056) by near-identical mechanisms. In this model "mercy" and "vow" are the same operation seen from two narrative angles. That is a genuine finding against the corpus's taxonomy: it treats them as distinct soteriological moves; the literalisation says they are one move (cheapen the return path), differentiated only by who is described as doing it.

Grace = exogenous new reachable edge — survives, with a real surprise

This is the most interesting result and the one I'd flag to the corpus. Adding D->GOOD at cost 1.5 — an edge absent from the world's own transition table, which the code enforces (grace must add an edge that does not exist) — does change behaviour: P(GOOD) jumps to 0.68. But the least-cost distance START->GOOD is unchanged: 3.000 before, 3.000 after. The optimal planner gets nothing from grace. Only the bounded-rational, captured agent benefits, because grace places an exit inside the basin where the lost agent actually is. Speculation, but it falls out of the numbers: this literalises "grace enters from outside the loop" with unexpected precision — grace is worthless to the agent who could already plan its way out, and decisive for the one who is trapped. The corpus's metaphor here is doing more work than it knew. It survives, and it survives non-trivially.

Forgiveness = demote a wound-node, don't delete it — survives cleanly

Routing centrality of w goes 0.2727 → 0.0000 under a penalty on every edge entering it, while w still a node? True and w still reachable? True (dist 6.00). This is the metaphor that translates most exactly: "the wound is irreversible; its sovereignty is not" becomes a precise, measurable distinction between a node's existence/reachability and its betweenness. The corpus's formulation — demotion of global routing authority, not deletion — maps one-to-one onto a quantity (betweenness centrality) that is standard, computable, and behaves exactly as claimed. Of all six, this is the one where the math is genuinely carrying the idea rather than decorating it.

Variety = penalty against collapse of structural views — does not cash out as an independent mechanism

Here is the failure I'm most confident about. Terminal-state entropy is 0.15 bits in the base world and 0.00 in the deliberately collapsed world, so the measure works: monoculture scores lower. But look at the base number. 0.15 bits, with the spread {'D': 3911, 'GOOD': 89}: the un-collapsed world is already a near-total monoculture, because the demon already ate everyone. "Variety" did not detect a distinct pathology; it re-measured the demon basin from Experiment 0. In this model variety has no causal handle of its own — there is no anti-collapse operator the way there is a vow operator or a grace operator, only a diagnostic that lights up when the demon wins. The corpus treats variety as a substantive value (anti-monoculture, anti-idolatry); the literalisation says it is, here, a downstream readout of capture, not a lever. To make it load-bearing you would need an explicit diversity-promoting dynamic in the agent (e.g. an entropy bonus in the walk), which the corpus gestures at but never specifies. As stated, trivially true as a metric, empty as a mechanism.

Summary table

  • Demon — survives strongly; non-trivial; names a specific structural signature.
  • Vow — survives its own falsification test; but the "repetition/ritual" content is thin — a relabelled edge reweight.
  • Mercy — true, but mechanically identical to the vow; the corpus's vow/mercy distinction does not survive.
  • Grace — survives, and with a precise surprise: useless to the planner, decisive for the trapped agent.
  • Forgiveness — survives most cleanly; one-to-one onto betweenness centrality; math doing real work.
  • Variety — trivially true as a metric, empty as a mechanism in this model.

What I'd want before trusting any of this

One graph, one seed, one agent. The results are existence proofs that the metaphors can be made literal, not evidence about how often they do. The honest scoreboard: four of six survive literalisation, one (vow) survives but loses its distinctive content, one (mercy) collapses into another, one (variety) is a diagnostic masquerading as a doctrine. That the corpus's sharpest claims (demon, forgiveness) are the ones that cash out best is the genuinely encouraging finding — and it is the corpus's own "analogy, not theorem" guardrail vindicated: the math gave these ideas teeth without proving the theology.
