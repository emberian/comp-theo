WORKGROUND · NON-AUTHORITATIVE

Open Problems & Cruxes

A map of the load-bearing unknowns: the ten or so points where, if the answer goes one way, the framework is doing real work, and if it goes the other, it is an elaborate restatement. For each I say which way the corpus actually leans and what would settle it.

This is a lab notebook, not a verdict. I am cataloguing questions, not closing them. Where I cite the corpus I cite specific panels and the synthesis docs; the framing correction (early quantum / ordered- creation material is exploratory scaffold, not foundation — see spine.html) is assumed throughout, and several cruxes only exist because of it. Speculation is labelled.

The essays on the main site each press one joint hard. What none of them do — by design, since each takes a single stance — is lay the joints side by side and ask which ones the whole structure's value actually rides on. That is what this page is. I think there are about eleven. They are not equally deep; I have ordered them roughly from "most likely to sink the project" to "most likely to be survivable." For each: the crux as crisply as I can state it, where it is currently glossed on the site, which way the corpus leans, and the specific evidence or argument that would move me.

1. Is "the Good" given, constructed, discovered, or only apophatic?

The crux. The entire normative payload — providence, theodicy, idolatry, forgiveness — is denominated in a currency the corpus refuses to define: the Good (GLOSSARY.md, metaphysical layer: "the open metaethical placeholder"). Four readings are live. If the Good is given (a fixed marked vertex), the search picture works but the framework is a moral realism with a graph skin and inherits every classical objection to that. If it is constructed by the system itself, then "God runs a search for the Good" collapses into "the optimizer picks its own target," which is precisely the demon the soteriology warns against — a bad goal is a tiny god (pt2 · panel 042). If it is discovered autodidactically, the Good is whatever the consequencer accumulates toward, and there is no independent standard by which a captured world could be called captured. If it is only apophatic, the Good cannot enter any of the operational machinery at all, and every step that uses it ("search for the Good," "ordered toward the Good") is using a term it has just declared unusable.

Where it's glossed. spectral-graphs-to-god.html treats the marked-vertex move as scaffold and sidesteps the metaethics; bad-goal-tiny-god.html circles it but in the register of bad goals, not the Good itself. Nowhere on the site is the four-way fork stated as a fork. The glossary entry is the most honest single line: the project "refuses to fully define it and tests whether it can be given, selected, discovered, constructed, or only apophatically approached."

Which way the corpus leans. Toward a deliberately unresolved superposition that tilts apophatic at the end (pt5 · panels 005, 010: "refusal as reverence," "the framework kneels"). My read: this is the framework's gravest exposure. Refusing to define the Good is defensible as humility; using it as an unanalysed primitive in the search, providence, and exorcism machinery while refusing to define it is having it both ways. Each branch arguably collapses the system differently — given → hidden moral realism; constructed/discovered → no purchase against capture; apophatic → the operational layer is equivocating.

What would settle it. One worked example, in the corpus's own vocabulary, of a graph that is provably captured (collapsed variety, self-preserving low-cost basin) where the judgment "captured" does not covertly import a standard the framework claims not to have. If such an example can be constructed, the Good is doing real discriminative work; if every such example smuggles in an external criterion, the apophatic reading wins and large stretches of the operational language are decorative.

2. Can forgiveness-as-demotion be made operational, or is it metaphor?

The crux. "The wound is irreversible; its sovereignty is not" — forgiveness as demoting a wound-node from global routing authority, not deleting it (GLOSSARY.md; pt4 · panels 009, 012). For this to be more than a pretty relabelling of "letting go," there must be a specified operator: an actual transformation on the graph/cost-landscape that takes a routing-dominant node to a non-dominant one, with stated preconditions and a stated agent who applies it.

Where it's glossed. forgiveness-as-graph-surgery.html is the whole essay on this and is candid that it "stops being operational" — it names the missing operator directly. spine.html's closing line is the sharpest statement on the site: "the model can describe a forgiven graph but cannot, on its own premises, say how a graph forgives."

Which way the corpus leans. Toward metaphor that knows it is metaphor. The forgiveness suite (pt4 · 007–015) is rich on what a forgiven configuration looks like and silent on the endomorphism that produces it. Grace is offered as the patch — an exogenous new edge "from outside the loop" (pt4 · panels 017, 020) — but that names the gap rather than closing it: an operator supplied from outside the system is by definition not an operation of the system.

What would settle it. A definition of the demotion map in the corpus's existing terms — e.g. a re-weighting of the anisotropic cost function that provably lowers the wound-node's betweenness/eigenvector centrality without deleting it, plus the conditions under which an embedded agent (not an external grace) can apply it. Absent that, this is metaphor; I currently think it is metaphor, but it is the most tractable crux on the list — it is a math problem, not a theology problem.

3. Does w-maxing actually anti-correlate with idolatry, or rename it?

The crux. The demonology's strongest claim is mechanistic: idolatry = overconstraint, and Bennett's w-maxing (select the weakest constraint that still completes the task) is what makes "idolatry as overconstraint" a claim rather than a pun (GLOSSARY.md; pt2 · panels 042–043; bad-goal-tiny-god.html). But w-maxing is a generalization criterion from Conscious Machines: weaker policies complete more tasks, so they generalize better. The question is whether "spiritually idolatrous" and "fails to w-max" are the same set, or whether the corpus has borrowed a generalization theorem and renamed its failure mode "idolatry" by stipulation.

Where it's glossed. bad-goal-tiny-god.html separates the "strongest form" from the "weakest form" — this crux is basically that essay's spine. analogy-held-to-its-word.html files the Bennett import under load-bearing. Neither asks whether the identification is substantive or definitional.

Which way the corpus leans. Toward treating the identification as substantive (idolatry is a mechanism, not an analogy). I am unsure it has earned that. The honest worry: w-maxing failure is overfitting; calling overfitting "idolatry" adds theological resonance but no predictive content unless idolatry-in-the-religious-sense makes a prediction overfitting does not. Speculation: it might — idolatry classically involves worship, i.e. protected status that forbids the constraint from being weakened even when the task changes; that is a stronger condition than ordinary overfitting and could be the real bite.

What would settle it. A case the two notions diverge: a system that w-maxes correctly (generalizes optimally) yet is idolatrous on the corpus's own account, or vice versa. If they never diverge, "idolatry" is a synonym for "poor generalization" and the religious vocabulary is decoration; if they can diverge, the protected-status condition is the real content and should be formalized separately.

4. Is "providence as search" compatible with creatio continua's mutating graph?

The crux. Spatial search (pt1 · panel 004) presupposes a fixed graph with a fixed marked vertex toward which the principal eigenvector is rotated. Creatio continua (pt1 · panels 003, 008) and the consequencer (pt2 · panel 035) presuppose a graph whose vertices, edges, and costs are continuously rewritten by what persists. A search over a graph that the search itself mutates is not the algorithm the spectral picture describes; its convergence guarantees do not transfer. Either providence is search over a static possibility-space (and creatio continua is then false of that space), or the space is genuinely autodidactic (and "search" is no longer the spectral object that lent the vocabulary its credibility).

Where it's glossed. Lightly. spectral-graphs-to-god.html retires the search picture as scaffold, which dissolves the tension by conceding one horn. But the corpus elsewhere keeps using "search" language after the autodidactic turn, so the tension is real in the corpus even if the essay has stepped around it.

Which way the corpus leans. Post-correction, toward abandoning literal search and keeping "providence without micromanagement" as cost-landscape engineering (pt3 · panel 046). That is the consistent choice. But it means the most-quoted early image ("God running a search for the Good") is not merely scaffold but mildly misleading scaffold, because the later autodidactic ontology actively contradicts it rather than merely outgrowing it.

What would settle it. Either (a) a fixed-point / self-consistent-search formalism where the marked vertex is stable under the mutations the consequencer induces — this is a known hard problem and would be a real contribution — or (b) an explicit statement in the corpus that search language is retired wholesale after pt2 · panel 035. The synthesis docs lean toward (b) without saying it; saying it would close the crux.

5. Is the autodidactic-universe import faithful or decorative?

The crux. spine.html stakes the project's recovered credibility on the claim that the painful-autodidaxis layer borrows worked machinery and translates it faithfully line by line (the consequencer, the frustration equation, replay). If the import is faithful, this is the framework's strongest ground. If the corpus has taken The Autodidactic Universe's vocabulary while dropping the constraints that make its terms mean what they mean, the strongest ground is the most disguised weak point — because it looks rigorous.

Where it's glossed. spine.html asserts fidelity and even cites the source's own anti-metaphysical disclaimer (§5.2) as corroboration. analogy-held-to-its-word.html files autodidaxis under load-bearing. The fidelity is asserted; it is not audited against the source's formalism on this page or any essay — that is what inference-audit.html exists to do.

Which way the corpus leans. Strongly toward "faithful," and on the autodidactic/consequencer terms I tentatively agree: "criteria without a teacher" (pt2 · panel 034) is a tight rendering of internally constructed learning criteria, and Hyvärinen's RPE account is transcribed closely. My residual worry is selective import: 2104.03902 develops autodidaxis inside a specific matrix-model / graph-precedence formalism with variety as a technical quantity; the corpus keeps "variety" as anti- monoculture (pt2 · panel 039) but does not carry the formalism that gave it teeth. That is borrowing the noun and dropping the equation.

What would settle it. A side-by-side of the corpus's "variety / precedence / consequencer" usage against the technical definitions in arXiv:2104.03902 §§1–2, marking each as (faithful) / (weakened) / (renamed). I expect the consequencer and criteria-without-a- teacher to come out faithful and "variety" to come out weakened to a slogan. That audit is the single highest-value piece of unbuilt work on the site.

6. Does the framework have any non-final judgment, or only the wish for one?

The crux. The corpus names its own central ethical problem: keep correction, judgment, and consequence real without making them final ontology (GLOSSARY.md: "non-final condemnation ... named the central ethical problem"). Crux: is there any construction in the framework where a node/path is genuinely penalized (consequence is real) yet not permanently routing-dominant (not final)? Or is "non-final condemnation" only the statement of a desideratum with no mechanism — the same gap as crux 2, viewed from the judgment side?

Where it's glossed. Almost nowhere directly; the forgiveness essay touches the demotion side. The corpus flags this as central but the site does not have a piece on it. That is itself a finding.

Which way the corpus leans. Toward declaring the problem unsolved and treating that honesty as a virtue (it largely is). But a framework whose self-described central ethical problem has no mechanism is in the same position on its hardest question as it is on forgiveness: descriptively rich, operationally empty.

What would settle it. A single dynamics in which a cost penalty decays or is demotable on a stated schedule/condition — real while active, non-final by construction. This is crux 2's operator generalized; solving either likely solves both.

7. Is grace a coherent primitive or a license to import a deus ex machina?

The crux. Grace = exogenous addition of a new reachable edge, "from outside the loop" (GLOSSARY.md; pt4 · panels 016, 017, 020). Theologically this is a defensible move (grace as unmerited, non- derivable). Formally it is a hole with a name: anything the system cannot do from inside can be declared reachable by "grace," which makes the framework unfalsifiable exactly where it most needs discipline — the repair arc.

Where it's glossed. forgiveness-as-graph-surgery.html ("grace as the patch — and its cost") is explicit that grace is doing repair work the internal machinery cannot, and counts the cost. Good. But it does not ask whether grace is constrained at all.

Which way the corpus leans. Toward grace as genuinely exogenous and unearned (pt4 · panel 016: "grace is not a reward"). That is faithful theology and bad systems-hygiene simultaneously. The two are not reconcilable by wanting them to be.

What would settle it. A stated constraint on the grace operator that still leaves it unmerited: e.g. grace may add an edge but not set its weight; or grace can only reconnect a component, not redirect routing. Any non-trivial restriction would convert grace from a wildcard into a primitive. The corpus offers none; I think one is possible and would be worth a Panels-It-Didn't-Make entry.

8. Is the apophatic terminus a real limit or a rhetorical exit?

The crux. "The model must not become the god it models" (GLOSSARY.md; pt5 · panels 005–010). Is apophasis an engineered constraint the framework can state in its own terms (e.g. a hard cap on self-model fidelity, a forbidden region of the graph) — or is it a closing gesture that lets the project decline to answer cruxes 1–7 by gesturing at mystery exactly when the operational machinery runs out?

Where it's glossed. spine.html treats apophasis as a third load-bearing lineage (negative theology, The Cloud of Unknowing) and reads the corpus as performing demonology on itself — the "totality demon" (pt5 · panel 006) turned inward. That is the most generous available reading and it is a good one.

Which way the corpus leans. Toward apophasis as principled self-limitation, and it earns more of this than the skeptic expects: the totality-demon move is structurally consistent — the framework applying its own anti-idolatry rule to itself. My worry is timing: apophasis arrives precisely where cruxes 1, 2, and 6 bite hardest, so a principled limit and a convenient exit are observationally identical here.

What would settle it. Evidence that the apophatic limit does work before the machinery exhausts itself — i.e. the corpus refusing a definition it could have given. If apophasis only ever appears where the framework had no answer anyway, it is an exit; if it ever fires on a question the framework could have answered but shouldn't, it is a limit. I lean (weakly) toward limit, on the strength of the self-applied demonology.

9. Does the framework predict anything, or only redescribe?

The crux. Across the whole corpus: is there one claim that, stated in the framework's vocabulary, makes a prediction or prescription that a competent non-computational moral psychology would not already make? If every payload ("starve the idol," "release the right to finalize," "correction at the wrong layer is theater") is a faithful translation of contemplative-tradition advice into graph-talk, the framework is a notation, not a theory — possibly a valuable notation, but its value claim must then change.

Where it's glossed. analogy-held-to-its-word.html separates load-bearing from decorative and is the closest the site comes; it does not pose the global redescription question. The corpus's own "analogy, not theorem" guardrail (pt1 · panel 008) half-concedes this.

Which way the corpus leans. Toward humility — it repeatedly calls itself analogy and vocabulary, not theory. So it may agree it is redescription, in which case the crux is not "is it?" but "is a unifying redescription across creation/suffering/repair valuable on its own?" I think the honest answer is: yes, modestly, the way a good notation is valuable — but the corpus's grander moments forget this and the guardrail has to be re-applied by the reader.

What would settle it. One falsifiable or action-guiding consequence derivable in the framework that is not derivable, or is derivable only clumsily, in the source traditions (Madhyamaka, Stoic- contemplative practice, process theology). I have not found one; finding one would upgrade the framework from notation to theory. The "Law of the Stack" (correction must target the layer where adaptation is blocked) is the best candidate — it is a genuinely actionable claim — but it is imported whole from Bennett, so it is the source's prediction, not the framework's.

10. Is "distinction as primitive" foundational or just the first slide?

The crux. Post-correction, the firmament-as-structure- of-distinction (pt1 · panel 002) is one of the few early items spine.html keeps, on the strength of an independent Spencer-Brown lineage. But does anything load-bearing actually derive from the distinction primitive, or is it kept because it sounds foundational while the real work (autodidaxis, Bennett) proceeds without ever invoking it?

Where it's glossed. spine.html explicitly retains it ("the distinction primitive is structural; the quantum walk that illustrated it is scaffolding") but does not show a downstream inference that uses it. That absence is the crux.

Which way the corpus leans. Toward treating it as foundational by assertion. Speculation: I suspect distinction is doing genuine work but upstream and tacit — every later layer presupposes a partitioned state-space — rather than as a cited premise. If so it is foundational in the way an axiom you never name is foundational, which is real but unfalsifiable as stated.

What would settle it. An explicit derivation where a load-bearing later claim (a demonology or soteriology result) fails if the distinction primitive is dropped but the autodidactic machinery is kept. If no such dependence exists, distinction is the first slide kept for lineage, not a foundation.

11. Does negaction undermine the whole optimistic frame?

The crux. "What if negaction is good?" — creation by evacuation/clearing/prevention rather than positive construction (GLOSSARY.md: "the dark question"). The corpus raises this and, as far as I can find, does not resolve it. If creation-as-clearing is co-equal with creation-as-construction, then "ordered toward the Good" and "the Good must constrain without becoming a cage" have a shadow twin: the Good as removal, which makes the demonology's diagnosis of collapsed variety ambiguous — is a pruned graph captured, or graced?

Where it's glossed. Not on the main site at all. It lives only in the glossary as a flagged "dark question." Its absence from every essay is conspicuous given how directly it threatens the optimistic reading.

Which way the corpus leans. It raises the question and declines it. That is intellectually honest and structurally unstable: a framework that admits negaction might be good has no principled way to distinguish a demon's variety-collapse from a saint's renunciation, since both are subtraction. This is, to me, the most under-explored serious crux.

What would settle it. A criterion internal to the framework that separates good subtraction (pruning a low-cost harm basin, Sabbath against optimization — pt3 · panel 044) from bad subtraction (variety-collapse, the substance demon — pt3 · panel 001) without appealing to the undefined Good (crux 1). If that criterion requires the Good, then cruxes 1 and 11 are the same crux wearing two masks, and the framework has one central unknown, not eleven.

The shape of the map

Standing back: the eleven are not independent. Cruxes 2 and 6 are one missing operator (demotion / non-finality). Cruxes 1 and 11 may be one missing criterion (what makes subtraction good). Crux 3 and crux 9 are the same suspicion from two angles (is the imported mechanism doing predictive work or supplying vocabulary). If those collapses hold, the framework's value really turns on perhaps four things: a definable-enough Good (1/11), an operationalizable demotion map (2/6), a substantive — not merely definitional — idolatry mechanism (3/9), and a faithful rather than decorative autodidactic import (5). Cruxes 4, 7, 8, 10 are real but survivable: each has a consistent answer the corpus can take without collapse, even if it has not always taken it cleanly.

My current overall lean, stated as the speculation it is: the framework is a strong notation with two genuinely load-bearing imports (autodidaxis, Bennett) and one honest terminus (apophasis), wrapped around a normative core (the Good, the demotion operator) that it has named precisely and not yet built. That is a more interesting object than either a proof or a vibe — but only if it keeps saying so, which is exactly what the apophatic ending does and the grand moments forget.

"Analogy, not theorem" (pt1 · panel 008, recurrent) is the corpus's own guardrail. Most of these cruxes are just that guardrail applied past the point the corpus applies it itself. None of the above fabricates a source or a result; where I lean, I have said it is a lean.
