WORKGROUND · NON-AUTHORITATIVE

The Strongest Version

If I grant this project its kindest readings, what is the tightest thing left standing — and what does each charitable reading cost?

This is a Workground note: my own working reconstruction, not the project author's position and not a settled scholarly claim. I argue with the corpus elsewhere on this site; here I am deliberately on its side. Speculation is labelled. Citations point to real material (parsed/pt*, the transcript, and the two arXiv sources the corpus actually leans on).

The brief I set myself

The harsh pass is easy and I will write it elsewhere. The harder and more useful exercise is adversarial generosity: assume the author is not confused, read every ambiguous move in its best light, and see what survives. My claim in this note is narrow. Computational theology is not a theory. Read at its best, it is a disciplined analogical theology — a controlled vocabulary for old theological problems, borrowed from learning theory and graph dynamics, that earns its keep when it generates non-obvious, criticisable reformulations. On that reading it is a research program, not a result, and it is a good one in a handful of specific places. I will name those places and price them honestly.

First: concede the framing correction, and notice it helps

The standing correction on this archive is that the early quantum-walk / Tamon material and the "ordered creation" subseries (pt1 · 001–043) are exploratory motivators, not a load-bearing foundation. The steelman does not resist this — it depends on it. The strongest version of the project is the one that drops the physics claim entirely. Nothing defensible here rests on creation literally being a quantum walk on a graph, or on the firmament being a real spectral object. Those panels are scaffolding the author themselves kicked away ("stop using bullshit mathemes," recorded as a mid-project stance marker in the glossary). What is left after the kick is not less; it is the actual subject: a theory of persistence, cost, and constraint in systems that learn. That pivot — from cosmology to learning — happens around the painful-autodidaxis turn (pt2 · 003 onward) and everything I find genuinely productive lives after it.

The tightest thesis that survives

Stated as conservatively as I can make it:

Many classical theological structures — providence, idolatry, the bondage of sin, forgiveness, grace — have a common abstract shape: they are claims about what a goal-directed, memory-bearing system makes cheap or reachable, and about who or what controls that landscape. Learning theory has precise, independently-developed vocabulary for exactly that shape (accumulated state with causal power; cost-weighted reachability; over-narrow objectives; basins of attraction). Putting the two in correspondence — never identity — yields reformulations that are sometimes sharper than the originals and, crucially, sometimes falsifiable as advice.

That last clause is the whole game. A theology that only re-describes is decoration. This one occasionally makes a claim you could act on and be wrong about. I will give the strongest examples, then the price.

The four moves I think are genuinely productive

1. The consequencer as causal memory — the load-bearing import

This is the strongest single move and it is not the project's invention, which is a point in its favour. "Consequencer" is Smolin–Tegmark et al.'s term in The Autodidactic Universe (arXiv:2104.03902): "A consequencer accumulates information from the past that is more influential to the future than is typical for other contents of the system" — a thermostat, a star, a gene, a bit, a knowledge graph. The corpus takes this precisely and runs it as a theological primitive (pt2 · 035, "what is kept accumulates… what accumulates influences… what influences becomes the world"; C t+1 = C t + ΔM place).

Why I think this earns its keep: it converts a vague pastoral claim ("the past has power over you") into a structural one with a mechanism and therefore a lever. A wound matters to the future only if it is stored as a consequencer — only if it has acquired routing power over later states (the corpus's own gloss, pt2 · 036 "pain becomes law only if stored"). That is not mush; it predicts where to intervene (at storage and at routing weight, not at the event) and it rhymes exactly with the source paper's observation that the choice of consequencer "defines the ontology of the learning system." The autodidactic move on top — a system whose criteria of learning are themselves internally constructed (2104.03902: "where a learning system constructs its own criteria") — is the honest engine behind the corpus's later "the universe goes to school." Read charitably, the theology is using a real, citable idea correctly.

Price: the source paper is a speculative physics proposal, not consensus, and it uses "consequencer" for thermostats and genes — morally weightless systems. Importing it to wounds and grace is a genuine analogical leap; the corpus is owed the move only if it never pretends the leap is entailment. It mostly doesn't (pt5 · 007, "structural kinship / ontological difference"), but the burden is permanent, not discharged once.

2. Idolatry as overconstraint — the best original reformulation

This is, to me, the most intellectually productive thing the project does, and the place where it is most nearly saying something new. The classical sin of idolatry is treating a finite thing as ultimate. The corpus reformulates it as a control-theoretic pathology: an over-narrow objective function that collapses variety and overconstrains the world that the agent can reach ("a bad goal is a tiny god," pt2 · 042; "the metric becomes an idol," pt2 · 043; idolatry as overconstraint "under sacred cover," glossary). The teenager-legible version — "the temples of the future won't ask for your soul, only your bandwidth" — is doing real work, not just being quotable: it identifies the failure as structural narrowness, not wrong belief.

Why it survives scrutiny: it is testable as a diagnosis. It says the signature of idolatry is measurable — collapsed variety, a single metric monopolising the cost landscape, sacrifice of everything not on the metric. That connects to a real literature: Goodhart's law, Hyvärinen's frustration account in Painful Intelligence (arXiv:2205.15409 — suffering as the error signal of a goal-bearing system under "uncontrollability, unpredictability, uncertainty"), and Bennett's w-maxing ("select the weakest constraint that still completes the task"). The corresponding cure follows from the same frame and is non-trivial: de-idolisation is the withdrawal of worship, i.e. de-weighting, not destruction (pt2 · 049, "what is not fed cannot rule"; reduce attention, certainty, replay, self-binding). That is a structurally different prescription from "believe the right thing," and it is the kind of claim that could be wrong.

Price: "overconstraint" only does work if "variety" and "the Good" are more than gestures. The corpus deliberately leaves the Good as an open metaethical placeholder (glossary; pt5 · 009, "God beyond the optimizer"). That apophatic refusal is intellectually honest, but it means "idolatry = overconstraint relative to the Good" cannot be fully cashed out — only the relative pathology (collapse of options the agent itself would endorse on reflection) is rigorous. That weaker version is the one I would actually defend, and it is still worth having.

3. Forgiveness as routing-demotion, not deletion

Here the analogy produces something I find genuinely sharp, and it is the cleanest example of the program working as designed. The corpus's line is "the wound is irreversible; its sovereignty is not" (glossary), unpacked across the Forgiveness Suite as: forgiveness does not erase the consequencer (the trace stays — "let truth remain, but not dominion"), it withdraws the wound-node's grant of final authority over future routing (pt4 · 009: "the wound may speak; it does not get to sign"; "release the right to finalize"; pt4 · 012: "from judge to witness," responsibility without self-hatred).

Why this is good: it dissolves a real and ancient dilemma. "Forgive and forget" is psychologically false and ethically dangerous (it erases the record that grounds justice and self-protection); "never forgive" freezes the wound as permanent governor. The demotion model is a genuine third option with operational content: keep the memory's evidential value, revoke its veto over which futures are reachable. That maps cleanly onto the consequencer frame (you change the weight and routing authority of a stored node, not its existence) and onto the corpus's later "non-final condemnation" — the central ethical problem it names: keeping judgment real without making it final ontology. This is the move I would most want a working ethicist to take seriously.

Price: "demote the node, keep the record" is a clean picture in a graph and a hard, underspecified discipline in a person. The analogy supplies the distinction (record vs. authority) but not the procedure; the corpus's R-step liturgies (recognise / release / reclaim / realign / receive) are mnemonics, not mechanisms. The steelman claim is only that the conceptual factoring is correct and useful, not that the practice is solved.

4. Providence as cost-landscape engineering — the deflationary win

The perennial problem of providence is the dilemma between a micromanaging God (which voids freedom and sharpens theodicy to the point of cruelty) and an absent one. pt3 · 046 reframes providence as the design of the cost landscape — topology and edge weights — such that good states are reachable within "the threshold of hope and ability," without dictating any single path (d(a,b) = min over paths of ∑ w(e), the tropical / least-action shape from the glossary). "Providence without micromanagement" is, I think, a legitimate small philosophical contribution dressed as decoration: it is a precise statement of a position (architect of priors and channels, not per-event intervener) that process theology and free-will theodicy gesture at but rarely formalise even this much. And it pairs honestly with its own dark twin — negaction, creation by clearing and prevention rather than positive construction — which keeps the frame from being merely consoling.

Price: the formula is illustrative arithmetic, not a model — there is no actual cost function, no actual graph, and the corpus's own guardrail ("analogy, not theorem") forbids treating d(a,b) as proving anything. The contribution is conceptual clarity about what kind of thing providence could be, nothing quantitative. That is still a contribution; it is just a smaller one than the typography implies.

Why this is a program and not a theory (the strongest framing)

The kindest accurate description is that the corpus is an exercise in what I would call controlled analogical theology. It has, to its credit, built its own guardrails into the object: "analogy, not theorem"; "correspondence, not collapse"; "correction at the wrong layer is theater"; the apophatic limit that "the model must not become the god it models" (pt5 · 005 "refusal as reverence"; pt5 · 010 "the framework kneels — a lamp, not the sun"). Most theological system-building lacks an internal stop condition. This one ships with one. The steelman is not "the analogies are true"; it is "the discipline of holding them as analogies is itself the method, and applied honestly it produces a handful of reformulations sharp enough to argue with." A research program is exactly a thing that generates criticisable conjectures and survives its own refutations of detail. By that Lakatosian standard the project clears the bar — modestly, in four places, at a stated price each.

The price of the whole charitable reading, stated once

I should not smuggle in hype, so here is the bill in full. (1) Every move above works only in the deflationary register; the moment any of them is read as physics, proof, or entailment, it fails, and the posters' Latin maxims and spectral diagrams constantly tempt that misreading — the aesthetic actively undercuts the epistemic humility the text professes. (2) The productive moves are imported, not derived: consequencer and autodidaxis from Smolin et al., frustration from Hyvärinen, w-maxing and the Law of the Stack from Bennett. The corpus's originality is in the theological recombination, which is real but narrower than the volume of material suggests. (3) The unfalsifiable core — God, the Good, grace as "exogenous edge from outside the loop" (pt4 · 017) — is untouched by any of this and is honest enough to say so; the steelman cannot make it say more. (4) Four good moves across ~210 panels is a low yield, and a generous reader has to actively discard a great deal of atmosphere to find them. The strongest version of this project is real, but it is small, borrowed at the load-bearing joints, and surrounded by far more material than it can support.

I still think the four moves are worth the dig. That is the most defensible thing I can say, and I am saying it on the project's behalf.

Speculative aside (mine, flagged): the single extension I think the strongest version should have drawn and didn't — forgiveness-as-demotion and idolatry-as-overconstraint are the same theorem read in two directions (de-weighting a node that holds too much routing authority). If that identity holds, soteriology and demonology are one operation under opposite signs. I cannot show this; I only note that the corpus's own vocabulary makes the conjecture statable, which is the most a program is supposed to do.
