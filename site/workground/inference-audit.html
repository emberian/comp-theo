WORKGROUND · NON-AUTHORITATIVE

The Inference Audit

I take the framework's seven central moves one at a time, write each as an explicit proof obligation, and mark the exact line where an analogy gets silently promoted to an entailment.

This is a lab notebook, not a verdict. I am being a hostile logician on purpose, and trying to be a fair one. Where I speculate I say so. The corpus's own guardrail — "analogy, not theorem" (pt5 · 007, Analogy Without Collapse) — is the standard I am holding it to, not a standard I am importing. I treat the early quantum-walk / Tamon material as an exploratory motivator per the project's own correction, so I do not score it as a load-bearing premise.

What an "inference audit" means here

Most of the moves in this corpus have the shape: there is a formal structure F with property P; there is a theological notion T that resembles P; therefore T behaves like F. That is fine as heuristic and dangerous as argument. For each move I want three things on the page: (1) the premises actually needed; (2) the hidden quantifier or equivocation — usually a "some" doing the work of an "all", or one word naming two things; (3) what would have to be independently true (not true-by-analogy) for the inference to go through. I will use a small tag, [promotion], to mark the precise sentence where similarity is upgraded to identity or entailment.

Move 1 — Distinction → World

Claim (pt1 · 002, Firmament = Structure of Distinction; pt1 · 014): the primitive creative act is the mark/cut; to create is first to differentiate; a lawful partition of a graph G=(V,E) is what makes "above/below, inside/outside" intelligible.

Premises as stated. (P1) Any intelligible relation requires distinguishable relata. (P2) Distinction can be modeled as a partition/boundary on a state graph. (P3) Therefore world-making is distinction-making.

Hidden quantifier. P1 is close to analytic and I grant it: relation presupposes some differentiation. But P1 says distinction is necessary; the move needs it to be sufficient — that instituting separability yields a world rather than merely being a precondition for describing one. The leap from "no world without distinction" to "distinction constitutes the world" is a converse error (necessary→sufficient). Spencer-Brown's Laws of Form supplies a calculus of the mark; it does not supply content, dynamics, or value, and Spencer-Brown does not claim it does.

[promotion] at "to create first is to differentiate; to differentiate is not to destroy relation, but to render relation intelligible" (pt1 · 002, Key claim). The sentence is true read as render intelligible (epistemic). It is used downstream as render existent (ontic). One word, "render," carries both.

What would have to be independently true. That the partition structure is not just a representation we impose to talk about a world but a generative cause of it. That is exactly Markovian constructivism's substantive bet, and nothing in the formalism discharges it; it is assumed, not earned. To its credit the corpus mostly treats the firmament as "condition under which," which is the defensible reading; the slide happens when later panels speak as if the cut itself does the creating.

Move 2 — Spatial search → Providence

Claim (pt1 · 004): a continuous-time quantum walk H = γA + |w⟩⟨w| rotates a globally spread principal eigenvector toward a marked vertex; read metaphysically, "search = passage from distributed amplitude to local manifestation," i.e. providence actualizing the Good.

Premises. (P1) There exists a formal dynamics that, from a global state, concentrates amplitude on a designated target. (P2) Providence is the actualization of a designated good. (P3) Therefore providence is (a) such a dynamics.

Hidden equivocation. Two: "marked" and "target." In spatial search the target w is given exogenously — the oracle term |w⟩⟨w| is a premise of the algorithm, not an output of it. The theological reading needs the Good to be found or at least non-trivially selected. So the formalism presupposes precisely what providence is supposed to accomplish. The corpus half-sees this — the panel's own caveat says "this does not mean God searches from ignorance… search [is] actualization, not lack of knowledge." Good. But that caveat removes the only feature that made search a model of anything: if the target is already fully known and the global state is already privileged toward it, "search" is decorative. You cannot both deny the epistemic content (no ignorance) and keep the dynamical content (localization is informative). Pick one.

[promotion] at "Search is a passage from distributed amplitude to local manifestation." The italic is turns a similarity of shape (diffuse→localized) into a claim that providence has this mechanism.

Independently true? Would need: a principled account of where the oracle |w⟩⟨w| comes from theologically, and why the metaethical target is structurally a rank-one perturbation rather than, say, a region, a measure, or something not representable as a marked vertex at all. None is given. Per the project's own correction this whole branch is an exploratory motivator, and I think that demotion is correct: the spatial-search picture is atmosphere, not a load-bearing inference.

Move 3 — Least action / tropical algebra → selection of the Good

Claim (pt1 · 035, Can God Be Just…): nature realizes least-action paths p* = arg min S[p]; goodness ≠ low action, justice ≠ efficiency; tropical/min-plus algebra (a⊕b=min(a,b), a⊗b=a+b) is "the algebra of least-action path selection," and the open question is how to compose action-minimization with good-maximization.

This is the strongest move in the corpus and I want to say so. It does not commit the analogy-to-entailment error in its core. The panel explicitly states "Nature tends to realize paths near p*, not because they are good, but because they are easy" and "Goodness ≠ low action." That is a disanalogy stated as a disanalogy — the right move. The tropical-semiring observation is a genuine, checkable fact: shortest-path / Viterbi-type optimization is min-plus linear algebra; that is textbook (Maslov dequantization; the tropical semiring), not a stretch.

Where it still owes something. The open problem ("compose S and G") is named, not solved, and the corpus is honest that it is named. The proof obligation it incurs: any composition argmin(αS − βG) just relabels the Good as a second cost term, at which point "least action vs. good-maximization" collapses into one optimization and the moral distinction the panel insisted on is gone. [promotion] risk lives not in the panels but in any reader who treats "we can compose them in an architecture" as "we have shown the Good is a cost." The panels stop short of that; later soteriology panels sometimes do not (see Move 5).

Independently true? What is needed and absent: an argument that the Good is even the kind of object that has a scalar representation closed under ⊗. If it is not (lexicographic, incommensurable, or only apophatically approachable — a possibility the corpus itself flags, GLOSSARY "Apophasis"), then the tropical machinery is the wrong category and the analogy is, at best, suggestive notation. Verdict: load-bearing math, honestly fenced, with the central composition left as an IOU. Fair.

Move 4 — Consequencer → vow / memory

Claim (pt2 · 035, The Consequencer; GLOSSARY): a "consequence accumulator" is a memory that has acquired causal power and reshapes future learning/ontology; bit, gene, star, archive, and vow are listed as instances; "stored salience becomes law." Cf. the autodidactic-universe sense of an accumulated evaluator (arXiv:2104.03902).

Premises. (P1) Some persistent records causally constrain later dynamics (true: genes, hysteresis, learned weights). (P2) A vow is a persistent record of commitment. (P3) Therefore a vow is a consequencer and "stored salience becomes law."

Hidden quantifier + equivocation. P1 is an existential ("some records have causal power"), used as if universal over the listed family. But the list bit → gene → star → vow equivocates on "memory": a star's "nuclear memory" is causally efficacious without any representation or normativity; a vow is normative and only efficacious through an agent who can hold it. Putting them in one column is a category blend. The slogan "stored salience becomes law" [promotion] upgrades a causal-influence claim ("affects the future") to a nomological one ("becomes law"). Memory biasing a trajectory is not memory becoming a law of the system; the corpus's own GLOSSARY line "pain becomes law only if stored" (pt2 · 036) shows it wants the strong reading.

Independently true? Would need a sharp criterion separating "a stored state that perturbs dynamics" from "a stored state that constitutes a rule," and an argument that vows clear the second bar by the same mechanism stars do. They almost certainly do not (one is enforced by physics, the other by a normative agent). The structural rhyme is real and useful as design vocabulary; as an inference it overreaches by one quantifier and one sense of "law."

Move 5 — w-maxing → anti-idolatry

Claim (GLOSSARY; pt2 · 042–043, A Bad Goal Is a Tiny God / The Metric Becomes an Idol): w-maxing — selecting the weakest constraint that still completes the task (Bennett, Conscious Machines) — generalizes better than simplicity-maxing; idolatry = overconstraint under sacred cover; "the Good must constrain without becoming a cage."

Premises. (P1) Weaker task-completing constraints generalize better (Bennett's technical claim, in a defined learning setting). (P2) Idolatry is over-narrow constraint treated as ultimate. (P3) Therefore the theological norm "avoid idolatry" is the engineering norm "prefer weakest sufficient constraint."

Hidden equivocation. "Weak/strong constraint" in Bennett is a precise notion (extension size of the policy that still satisfies the task within a model). "Idolatry as overconstraint" uses "constraint" morally and theologically — fixing the absolute too narrowly. These are homonyms. The inference treats a generalization theorem about predictive policies as a normative theorem about worship. [promotion] at "idolatry = overconstraint": the equals sign is doing illicit work; it should read "idolatry is illuminated by overconstraint." The corpus's late panels (pt5 · 007) actually endorse exactly this weaker reading ("close enough to illuminate, far enough to respect"), which I think is the right one and is in tension with the earlier equals-sign framing.

Independently true? Would need: that the theological "task" (the Good, salvation, faithful life) is even well-typed as a task with a satisfaction predicate over which constraint-strength is defined. If the end is not finitely specifiable (again the corpus's own apophatic limit), Bennett's result has no purchase on it — generalization bounds presuppose a task distribution. So: a striking and probably fertile analogy (over-optimization really does deform systems), but not an entailment. The diagnostic value ("watch for collapsed variety") survives even if the formal bridge does not.

Move 6 — Wound-node demotion → forgiveness

Claim (GLOSSARY; pt3 · 048–050, The Ledger of the Wound; pt4 · 012): "the wound is irreversible; its sovereignty is not" — forgiveness as demoting a wound-node from global routing authority, not deleting it.

Premises. (P1) In a routing/graph model a node can be reachable yet stripped of high betweenness (no longer on most paths). (P2) Forgiveness keeps the wound real but removes its governance over future action. (P3) Therefore forgiveness is wound-node demotion.

Assessment. This is the most internally disciplined of the theological moves and it is careful exactly where Move 1 was careless: it explicitly refuses deletion ("irreversible") and only claims a change in authority, which maps cleanly to centrality/priority rather than presence. The hidden assumption is small but real: that "sovereignty over routing" is a faithful gloss of what forgiveness releases. Counter-model: some accounts of forgiveness require a change in the relation (reconciliation), not merely a re-weighting inside one agent's policy graph; pt4 · 013 (Forgiveness Requires Boundaries) shows the corpus knows forgiveness ≠ restored access, which is consistent with demotion-not-deletion. So the analogy is offered as analogy and the disanalogies are partly self-flagged.

[promotion] only mild, at the GLOSSARY's "forgiveness as demoting a wound-node" — the "as" is fine; it tips over only if a reader infers that doing the graph operation constitutes having forgiven. Forgiveness may require the demotion without the demotion sufficing for it (necessary, not sufficient — the same converse error as Move 1, but here the corpus does not actually assert the sufficiency).

Independently true? Would need an argument that the morally salient content of forgiveness is exhausted by intra-agent re-prioritization. I doubt it is exhaustive, but the model is a legitimate component account and is presented with appropriate modesty.

Move 7 — Exogenous edge → grace

Claim (pt4 · 017, Grace Enters From Outside the Loop; pt4 · 020, Grace as Nonlocal Repair; GLOSSARY): grace is the exogenous addition of a new reachable edge; "the loop cannot save itself"; repair can begin at a distant node and still mend the whole network.

Premises. (P1) A closed transition system cannot reach states outside its reachable set by its own dynamics (true by definition of closure/reachability). (P2) Grace is unmerited and not generated by the system. (P3) Therefore grace = an externally added edge expanding the reachable set.

Hidden move — it is partly definitional, which is both its strength and its tell. P1 is a tautology about closed systems; it does real argumentative work only if we have independently established that the moral/soteriological situation is a closed reachability system. That premise is smuggled. If the "loop" is not provably closed (e.g., if ordinary repentance, community, or skillful means already enlarge reachability — which the corpus elsewhere asserts, pt3 · 042 "mercy changes the weights"), then "only an exogenous edge can help" is false on the corpus's own commitments. There is an internal tension: Moves 5–6 say practices reshape constraint/reachability from within; Move 7 says the loop cannot change without an outside edge. Both cannot be unrestrictedly true.

[promotion] at "The loop cannot save itself; grace interrupts" (pt4 · 017) — a definitional fact about closed graphs is presented as a substantive fact about this graph. The equivocation is on "loop": the closed-system "loop" (formal) vs. the psychological/sinful "loop" (lived), which is not known to be closed.

Independently true? Would need a non-circular argument that the relevant system is genuinely closed under all immanent operations — exactly the classical nature/grace question, on which the formalism is silent. "Nonlocal repair" (pt4 · 020) is the cleaner sub-claim: graph diffusion really can mend distant nodes, and that is a fair illustration; it does not by itself entail closure, so it does not establish the necessity of grace, only its possibility shape.

Scorecard (my read, contestable)

  • Necessary→sufficient (converse) errors: Move 1 (distinction), latent in Move 6 — but Move 6 doesn't assert the converse, so only Move 1 actually commits it.
  • Equivocation on a key term: Move 2 ("target/marked"), Move 4 ("memory/law"), Move 5 ("constraint"), Move 7 ("loop"). These are the four places one word does two jobs.
  • Honestly fenced, inference not overclaimed: Move 3 (the disanalogy is stated outright) and Move 6 (deletion explicitly refused). These are where the corpus's own "analogy, not theorem" discipline is actually operating.
  • Smuggled premise: Move 7 (closure of the system) and Move 2 (exogenous oracle) — both presuppose precisely what the theology is meant to deliver.

Speculative meta-claim: the corpus is at its most rigorous in the soteriology arc (Moves 3, 6) and at its loosest in the cosmogonic arc (Moves 1, 2). My guess is that this tracks the project's own course-correction — the early "ordered creation" / quantum-walk material was motivator-grade and the authors later said so; the discipline visibly improves once the "analogy without collapse" guardrail (pt5 · 007) is in force. That would be a point in the project's favor: it caught its own worst inferences.

Where I might be wrong: I have been strict about "necessary vs. sufficient" and about homonyms, which is the fair part of hostile logic. The unfair temptation I tried to resist is treating every analogy as a failed proof; several of these (3, 6, and the diagnostics of 5) are explicitly offered as analogies and should be judged as such. I have not independently re-derived Bennett's w-maxing result or the tropical-semiring identity from primary sources here; both are stated in the corpus and the latter is standard, but a fuller audit should check Bennett, Conscious Machines directly.
