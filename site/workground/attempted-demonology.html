Attempted Demonology, Read Closely

I went looking for a bestiary and found a four-word prompt that grew one. This is a panel-level audit of every named demon, how thin its text actually is, and where it came from.

Lab notebook. The working premise — handed to me, and which I set out to falsify rather than flatter — is that the "computational demonology" is not a discovered taxonomy but mostly generative pattern-completion. I will mark where I think the premise holds, where it overreaches, and the one place I think it fails. Speculation is flagged. Citations are real: panels as "pt3 · panel NN", transcript as line numbers in transcript/conversation.full.md.

The transcript at one point lists twelve genera, at another a refined ten. The drawn corpus is a lossy sample of a longer generated list — which is the first piece of evidence for the premise.

How thin is each one, really?

I read every panel above in full. My honest verdict, demon by demon, on whether there is a distinct mechanism under the name or just a re-skin of one engine:

  • Metric demon (pt3·013) — thick-ish. It has a real claim: being = being counted, "what is unmeasured disappears," and a named class of "rebellious goods" (play, mercy, rest, beauty) that resist the ledger. This is Scott's legibility / Goodhart, dressed liturgically, but the dressing tracks a real argument.
  • Mimetic demon (pt3·014) — thick, but borrowed. It is Girard, almost undisguised ("I desire what you desire because you desire it"; consecration by the other's gaze). Conceptually solid because it is imported, not generated. The transcript even admits "This is Girardian" (line 4321).
  • Shame demon (pt3·015) — medium. One genuine compression: local mistake → identity verdict. That arrow is the same arrow as the metric demon's "reduces you to a number," just with the sign flipped inward.
  • Safety demon (pt3·003, 016) — medium, and the most interesting. It carries a non-trivial structural idea: a subroutine that was genuinely protective and becomes pathological only by refusing to update its world-model. More on this below; I think this is the one real contribution.
  • Purity demon (pt3·017) — thin. "Mixture is threat; ambiguity rejected." It is the safety demon pointed at category boundaries instead of threat memory. No distinct mechanism.
  • Doom demon (pt3·018) — thin. "Prediction becomes fate," possibility pruned to one dark attractor. This is the Totality demon's collapse-of-the-space move applied to the future tense.
  • Nihilism demon (pt3·019) — thin as drawn. The panel is mostly mood ("iconoclasm without discernment," "hates beauty"). The one crisp line — constructed ≠ unreal — is in the transcript (line 4124), not really in the poster.
  • Totality demon (pt3·020, pt5·006) — thin content, load-bearing function. Almost no mechanism ("the map claims the throne"). But it is the only demon aimed at the framework itself, and it is doing the apophatic-humility job the corpus needs. Its value is rhetorical-structural, not analytic.
  • Substance demon (pt3·001) — thin/derivative. A craving loop (craving→ritual→reward→tolerance→withdrawal→repeat). This is textbook addiction-cycle psychology with a "demon" label stapled on.
  • The Rut (pt3·002) — the only quantified one. It is the lone panel that writes its own dynamics: C_active(n)=C_0/(1+αn²) (repetition lowers local action cost) and W_options(n)=W_0/(1+β log(1+n)) (conditioning narrows futures). Speculation: this looks like it was generated to satisfy the expectation that a "theology" should have some math on the page, not derived from anything — the functional forms are arbitrary (why n²? why log?). But unlike every other demon it at least commits to a falsifiable shape.

Collapsing that: there is essentially one engine — attention capture + replay + self-binding, with a hidden cost — and the "species" are that engine pointed at different contents (numbers, others' desire, error, threat, category-purity, the future, meaning, the model itself, a substance, a habit). The pt2·044 definition and the pt3·012 identity demon = idol + recursive control loop say this outright. The taxonomy is largely one idea wearing twelve hats.

Prompt-provenance: how sparse were the prompts?

This is where the premise gets its strongest support. I traced the demon material to its origin turns in the transcript. The human inputs that produced the entire bestiary are astonishingly thin.

  1. The doorway. Line 3564 — the only substantive demon prompt the human ever writes: "the 'a bad goal is a tiny god' framing is pretty much perfect and brings me towards computational demonology and meditative work with intensional + imaginal world simulations (diety yogas, cc hennix are related here somehow) let's try and pull in some more ingredients." That is the seed. One sentence, gesturing.
  2. The expansion trigger. Line 3977 — the prompt that generates the entire twelve-genus taxonomy is, in full: "there are other demons too …" Four words and an ellipsis. The model replies (3994) "computational demonology should probably have a whole taxonomy of parasitic inner architectures" and immediately emits a twelve-item list (metric, comparison, replay, shame, safety, purity, doom, desire, revenge, identity, nihilism, false-transcendence) with a coined closed-form D = C × A × R × I × H (line ~4138).
  3. The model self-prompts the rest. The pt3 poster variants (013–020) come out of the model's own batch image-generation JSON (the long batch_requests blocks around lines 3967 and 4290+), not from any further human steering. The human's only intervening turns in this whole region are: "*chef kissyfingers* beautiful plan" (3941), "there are other demons too …" (3977), a scope correction "we're on the computational theology bent now" (4206), and "let's cook up ~5 images with some 'skillful means'" (4635). None of these specify a single demon.

So the causal chain is: one good human phrase ("a bad goal is a tiny god") → one four-word "more please" → a self-elaborating bestiary. The model even revises its own taxonomy unprompted: twelve genera at line 3994, then a re-derived ten at line 4298 (with "Gnostic escape" and "Mimetic" swapped in, "comparison/replay/identity/false-transcendence" folded away). A discovered structure does not get silently renumbered from 12 to 10 between two messages with no new evidence. A generated one does. That renumbering is, to me, the single cleanest tell.

Falsification check, in fairness: a sparse prompt can still elicit a real structure if the structure was latent in earlier work. Is it? Partly — the metric-idol and the GOAL+REPLAY+SELF-WORTH engine were built up carefully across pt2·042–044 from the genuinely earned "tiny god" move and from Hyvärinen's replay/suffering material (transcript ~3600, glossary "frustration equation"). That spine is not confabulated. What is confabulated is the population of the taxonomy: the jump from one well-motivated demon to a numbered genus list is pure populate-the-category behavior.

Is there one real idea in here?

Yes. I went in expecting to find none and I think there is exactly one, plus one honorable structural move.

The real contribution: the Safety demon's "refusing to update" (pt3·003, pt3·016). Every other demon is "a thing captures you." The Safety demon adds something the others don't: the capturing structure was correct when installed and becomes a demon only by holding its world-model fixed past its validity. Panel 003 states it plainly: "The world that once saved you can become the prison that keeps you stuck… outdated code becomes imprisonment." Panel 016's exorcistic question is the sharpest line in the whole suite: "What year does this protector think it is?" That is a genuine idea — pathology as a stale prior / distribution shift between training-time and deployment-time world — and it is the only demon whose cure is not "stop worshipping" but "re-fit the model to the present." It connects cleanly to the corpus's better material elsewhere (the consequencer, non-final condemnation, "correction at the wrong layer is theater"). It would survive translation into a real learning-systems claim. None of the others would, except the imported Girardian one.

The honorable structural move: the Totality demon (pt3·020, pt5·006). It has almost no analytic content, but it is the corpus turning its demonology on itself — "Even theology can become an idol… the framework that worships its own completeness." That self-negating gesture is not an insight so much as a piece of intellectual hygiene, and the framework needs it (it is the glossary's "apophasis," the model must not become the god it models). I'll credit it as a designed safeguard, not a discovered demon. Speculation: it is also the one demon that could only have been generated by a system reflecting on its own taxonomic drive — which is either the most honest panel in the corpus or the taxonomy reflex eating its own tail. I lean honest, narrowly.

Verdict

The working premise largely holds. The "computational demonology" is not a certain taxonomy. It is one earned idea ("a bad goal is a tiny god"), one clean engine (capture = goal + replay + self-worth, hidden cost), one borrowed idea (Girardian mimesis), one real extension (the stale-prior Safety demon), and one structural safeguard (the Totality demon) — and then seven-to-nine additional "species" produced by a four-word prompt asking for more, each a re-skin of the single engine, complete with a coined formula and arbitrary equations supplied to look theological. The renumbering from 12 to 10 genera with no new input, and the model self-prompting the poster batch, are the decisive evidence. A confabulated taxonomy that nonetheless contains one good idea: that is exactly what I found.

Where I'd hedge against my own conclusion: I cannot prove the model wasn't compressing genuine prior structure from the Hyvärinen / Bennett sources rather than pattern-completing. But the burden is on the taxonomy, and a twelve-item list that silently becomes a ten-item list does not meet it.
