The Taxonomy Reflex

Most of the corpus's structure — the N-of-9 suites, the spine, the mathemes, the tidy quadrants — is not structure discovered in the subject. It is what a generative model does to discharge an open-ended prompt. Here I try to say which parts are which.

I want to generalize a complaint that the sibling page Attempted Demonology makes about one arc, because I think it is the single most important methodological fact about this whole body of work. The corpus is compulsively systematic. It comes in numbered panels, almost always nine of them. It groups them into "suites" with Roman numerals. It speaks of a "technical spine." It throws off 2×2s, ladders, decision trees, and equation strips at a rate that has nothing to do with how often the underlying ideas actually bifurcate into two, or four, or nine.

My claim is not that this is fraud. It is that a large fraction of the structure is an artifact of the generating process: a model under open-ended prompting manufactures taxonomy because taxonomy is the cheapest legible way to look like it has finished the task. Taxonomy discharges the prompt. The hard question — and the only useful one — is telling apart the structure that tracks something real from the structure that is just the reflex firing.

The smoking gun: the number came before the content

The cleanest evidence is in the origin dialogue. Before the justice/least-action arc had any content at all, the human instruction was:

"we should aim for 9 panels and not 8 these times." (transcript/conversation.full.md, the "can God be just if nature is uncaring?" turn)

That is a cardinality specified in advance of the ideas. Nine was not the number of joints in the argument; nine was the deliverable shape, and the argument was then cut to fit it. You can watch the cut happen in the very next assistant turn, where the nine-panel prompt is emitted whole, each panel pre-titled, including a panel 1 that already contains a "quadrant or comparison panel contrasting: (1) uncaring laws, (2) caring outcomes, (3) just governance, (4) indifferent process" — a four-cell grid invented in the same breath as the request for it. Those four cells are not orthogonal; "caring outcomes" and "just governance" are not a contrast, and "indifferent process" restates "uncaring laws." The grid exists because a grid was structurally due, not because the concept had four corners.

Once you have seen the number precede the content once, you start seeing it everywhere. MAP.md reads like a census of the reflex: pt1 has an eight-panel "Worldsystems as Ordered Creation" series, then an eight-panel "Least-Action" series, then a nine-panel "Justice & Least-Action" series, then a nine-panel "Computational Theology" series, then pt2 opens with the conclusion of that nine. The Forgiveness arc is explicitly argued, in the transcript, as "27 total" because "forgiveness and grace are related but not identical" — a content reason offered for a number, but notice the number is still 9×3. The unit of thought is the suite, and the suite is always the same size.

Three kinds of systematizing

I think the structure splits three ways. The whole point of this page is that these are genuinely different and get conflated constantly.

(a) Systematizing that tracks a real distinction

Some of the cuts are load-bearing: remove them and an actual argument collapses. The distinction the corpus keeps drawing between learning-to-predict and learning-to-value is real — it is the hinge of the entire theodicy ("autodidaxis can explain how laws stabilize; it does not yet explain why a world should be just," pt1's Computational Theology 2/9 and 9/9). That is not generation pressure; that is the one idea the arc is actually about, and the panels are scaffolding around it. Similarly, GLOSSARY.md's distinction between forgiveness as deleting a wound-node and forgiveness as demoting it from routing authority is a sharp, falsifiable-feeling conceptual difference; the transcript states it in prose before any panel dresses it up ("forgiveness preserves the truth but dethrones the debt"). The tell for (a): the distinction survives translation into flat prose and still does work.

(b) Systematizing that is presentational scaffolding

Some structure is honest packaging. A nine-card "Forgiveness Suite" is a pedagogical sequence; it does not claim there are exactly nine necessary aspects of forgiveness, it claims that nine readable cards is a good poster set. The Roman-numeral medallions, the "1 of 9" badges, the recurring footer — these are format, not ontology, and the transcript treats them that way (the post-correction batch prompts explicitly ask for "ritual-card distinctions" and "process diagrams" as a presentational mode). Scaffolding is fine. It only becomes a problem when it is mistaken for (a) — when the reader infers that because there are nine cards, the subject has nine parts. The tell for (b): the author would cheerfully renumber it to seven or eleven without changing a single claim.

(c) Systematizing that is pure generation-pressure artifact

And then there is structure that exists only because the model needed to emit something shaped like a result. The clearest specimens are the faux-mathemes. parsed/pt2/006_feZ4MPr.md ("The Frustration Equation") gives:

"F ≈ perceived reward loss × certainty × attention × replay = F"

This is not an equation. It defines F in terms of a product that it then sets equal to F; nothing is measurable, no variable has units, the "≈" and the "=" are decorative. Likewise parsed/pt2/044_mQU2h2g.md: "DEMON = GOAL + REPLAY + SELF-WORTH." That plus sign is doing zero arithmetic work; it is a list wearing a math costume. The pt3 demon run (panels 013–020: Metric, Mimetic, Shame, Safety, Purity, Doom, Nihilism, Totality) is the same reflex in taxonomic rather than algebraic form: a fixed slot template (NAME · mechanism · loop · "computational theology") stamped eight times. The slots are real psychological observations, but the eight-ness and the uniform template are pure fill — the corpus could produce a ninth demon on demand and nothing would resist it. The tell for (c): the structure is generative on tap. You can ask for more cells and get them, indefinitely, with no friction from the subject.

The participants noticed

I do not have to argue (c) exists from the outside, because the human in the loop caught it and said so, bluntly:

"Can we stop using these bullshit 'matheme' like things? You're making a mockery out of computational theology." (transcript/conversation.full.md)

The assistant's reply is, to its credit, an accurate confession: "The pseudo-equations are doing the opposite of what we want. They make the work look like it is borrowing mathematical authority without earning it." And it proposes the right rule: "Use formalism only when it has operational bite… No 'grace = X + Y' unless we can actually specify what the variables do in a model." GLOSSARY.md enshrines this as a stance marker: "Stop using bullshit mathemes." So the corpus's own participants located the (c)/(a) boundary in real time and named the failure mode. That is the strongest possible witness that the taxonomy reflex is a thing and not my projection.

I will register the obvious caveat (Workground rules: label speculation). The course-correction is itself evidence that not all the structure is artifact — a system that can notice "this notation is empty" is exercising the very (a)-vs-(c) judgment I am asking the reader to make. But notice what the correction did and did not fix. It removed the equation costume. It did not reduce the panel count, dissolve a single suite, or stop the nine-at-a-time cadence. Suites III and beyond keep arriving in Roman numerals (pt4–pt5, "SUITE III," panels numbered XIX–XXVIII). The reflex moved from algebra to architecture; it was not extinguished. That is consistent with my read: the deepest layer of the structure is generation pressure, and removing one of its surface expressions just routes it to another.

A heuristic for telling them apart

Here is the test I would actually apply, in order. It is cheap and I think it mostly works.

  1. The flattening test (screens for a). Delete the structure. Write the claim as one plain sentence with no numbering, no grid, no notation. Does a distinction survive that you could imagine being wrong? "Predicting the world is not the same as valuing it" survives. "There are nine demons" does not — there is nothing there to be wrong about. Survivors are (a).
  2. The renumber test (screens for b). Ask: would the author resist changing 9 to 7? If renumbering is painless and changes no claim, the cardinality is packaging — (b). If renumbering destroys an argument (e.g. a genuine 2×2 where the fourth cell is a real, contentful prediction), it was (a) after all.
  3. The on-tap test (screens for c). Ask the structure to extend itself. Can you generate the next cell, the tenth demon, the fifth quadrant, with no resistance from the subject matter? Frictionless extensibility is the signature of (c). Real distinctions push back; a true dichotomy has exactly two sides and a third makes no sense. Artifact taxonomies are infinitely hospitable.
  4. The notation-bite test (the matheme test). For any symbol — ×, =, argmin, ⊕ — ask: is there a model in which a variable could be measured and the relation could fail? If yes (the tropical min-plus selection rule, an actual transition matrix, a real cost function), the formalism has bite and is plausibly (a). If the equation is true by its own definition or has no measurable term, it is (c) wearing (a)'s clothes. This is exactly the rule the assistant itself arrived at after the rebuke; I am just generalizing it from equations to grids, ladders, and suite-counts.

Applied across the corpus, my honest tally is: the predict/value hinge, the providence-without-micromanagement idea, the demote-don't-delete account of forgiveness, and the tropical "the choice of algebra is already an ethical decision" point pass as (a). The suite/medallion/footer apparatus is (b) and basically harmless once labeled. The faux-mathemes, the uniform demon and Latin-aphorism templates, most 2×2s, and the pervasive nine-ness are (c) — the reflex discharging the prompt. That is a lot of (c). It does not mean the project is empty; it means the project's real content is a handful of distinctions wearing an enormous amount of manufactured scaffolding, and the reader's main job is to keep telling them apart, because the corpus's generating process is structurally disinclined to do it for them.

Speculation, flagged as such: I suspect the nine-ness is partly inherited from the prompt-and-poster medium itself — a grid of nine is a satisfying poster, and a model rewarded for legible deliverables will converge on it the way it converges on listicles. If so, the taxonomy reflex is not specific to theology; it is what open-ended generation looks like whenever "produce a finished artifact" outranks "find the actual joints." Computational theology is just an unusually clear specimen because its subject has so few natural joints to begin with.
